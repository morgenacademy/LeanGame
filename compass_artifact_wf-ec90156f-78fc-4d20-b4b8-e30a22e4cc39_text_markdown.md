# The LEGO Lean Simulation Game: The Essence and the Mechanics

## TL;DR
- **The LEGO Lean simulation works because it makes abstract Lean principles physically and emotionally undeniable.** Participants are not *told* that batching is wasteful — they *feel* the chaos of Round 1 (piles of work-in-process, idle hands, a frustrated customer, finger-pointing) and then *feel* the calm of flow in later rounds, so the lesson lands viscerally and almost unconsciously *before* it is ever named on a slide.
- **The mechanics are remarkably consistent across variants.** Teams build simple LEGO models (houses, cars, fire trucks, airplanes, advent-calendar figures, apartments) across multiple timed rounds; Round 1 uses large batches and push scheduling, and successive rounds introduce smaller batches, one-piece flow, pull/kanban, WIP limits, work cells, and kaizen. Hard numbers (lead time, throughput, WIP, defects, profit) are tracked, and the dramatic before/after gap is the teaching engine.
- **Two families dominate.** (1) Generic Lean/manufacturing flow games — the Sato & Trindade "Lego Lean Game," Velaction's Lean Flow Exercise, FlowCar, the "Learning Lean with LEGO" (3L) academic game, and the MTa Stockless Challenge; and (2) Lean *construction* simulations — Villego (now rebranded Plan2Flow), LEAPCON, LEBSCO, the DPR Pull Planning Game, plus the dice-based Parade of Trades. Use a generic flow game to teach flow-vs-batch and pull-vs-push; use Villego or LEAPCON to teach pull planning and cross-trade collaboration.

---

## Key Findings

1. **The core pedagogy is experiential learning (Kolb's cycle), not instruction.** Participants have a concrete experience (a round), reflect on it (debrief), form concepts (the facilitator names the principle *after* they have felt it), then experiment (next round). The repeated round → debrief → round structure is a deliberate Kolb loop typically run 3–6 times. A poorly debriefed simulation "stops at the first stage and produces entertainment rather than learning."

2. **The intended "aha" is discovery, not persuasion.** The best facilitators design for self-generated insight. Karl Scotland (author of the LEGO Flow Game) states the goal is to get participants "to reflect on the strengths and weaknesses of all the rounds and policy types, rather than show e.g. kanban to be the best."

3. **The emotional arc is the mechanism, not a side effect.** Documented shifts run excitement → tension/frustration/rushing/blame in the batch-push round → calm, idle waiting, and clarity in the flow/pull round. One practitioner sums it up: "People laugh. They compete. They get frustrated. Then they improve. That emotional connection is what makes the learning stick."

4. **The quantitative before/after gap is dramatic and constitutes the proof.** In the Villego construction simulation, every team flipped from a net loss in Round 1 to a net profit in Round 2, with build time roughly halved and productivity doubled-to-tripled. Research on the closely related Airplane Game found that **approximately 70% of the productivity gain is attributable to the mechanistic benefits of lean and less than 30% to non-mechanistic effects** such as the learning curve and Hawthorne effect (Rybkowski et al., "Investigation into the Nature of Productivity Gains Observed During the Airplane Game," *Lean Construction Journal*, 2012) — a crucial debrief point to pre-empt the "we just got better with practice" objection.

5. **Variants share a skeleton but target different concepts.** Manufacturing flow games target the 7/8 wastes, push vs. pull, WIP, kanban, work cells, takt time, line balancing, 5S, and kaizen. Construction games (Villego, LEAPCON, Parade of Trades) target pull planning, reliable promising, collaboration across trades, and the impact of variability and dependence on throughput.

## Details

### ASPECT 1 — THE ESSENCE: What participants should experience and discover

#### Why the simulation is so effective as a teaching tool
Lean concepts are experience-based and context-dependent — hard to transmit with slides. Simulation overcomes "Lean fatigue" in the classroom by giving learners a concrete, tactile, emotionally engaging experience before the abstractions can make sense. LEGO is ideal because it is cheap, universally familiar, fast to assemble and disassemble (which enables multiple rounds), visually obvious (piles of bricks literally *are* visible inventory and waste), and non-threatening — mistakes carry no real-world cost, creating the psychological safety (Edmondson) that is a precondition for taking interpersonal risks and learning. The hands-on, "think with your hands" quality engages embodied cognition and converts tacit understanding into something the body remembers.

#### The principles participants should FEEL and DISCOVER (not just hear)
- **Flow beats batch.** When work moves one piece at a time, the first finished unit appears far sooner, lead time collapses, and WIP shrinks — participants *see* the difference in time-to-first-delivery and in the size of the piles between stations.
- **Push creates waste; pull creates calm.** In the push round, upstream stations overproduce, inventory mounts, and customers go unserved despite frantic effort. In the pull round, stations build only when signaled by downstream demand — some workers stand idle, which itself becomes a discovery about line balance and *flow efficiency vs. resource efficiency*.
- **WIP limits feel counterintuitive but improve flow.** Limiting work-in-progress (e.g., a WIP limit of 1 per stage) feels restrictive, yet participants discover it smooths flow and shortens lead time — an embodiment of Little's Law.
- **Local optimization ≠ global optimization.** "Point speed and productivity do not matter — throughput does." A fast individual station that piles up inventory helps no one.
- **Quality and defects.** Batches hide defects until the whole batch is inspected; flow exposes a defect immediately (the next worker catches it), so rework is contained to a single unit.
- **Overproduction as the cardinal waste.** In Villego, a student who placed blocks early "to use up" remaining time then realized his blunder and, "in a disgusted tone," exclaimed, "So that is an example of overproduction, right?" — which the researchers record as verbalizing "the 'aha moment' for the entire group."
- **Collaboration and reliable promising.** Especially in construction variants, participants discover that siloed, self-optimizing, blame-oriented behavior (Round 1) is far worse than collaborative, customer-value-focused, promise-based planning (Round 2).

#### The emotional/experiential shifts between rounds
The Villego research (Warcup, R. & Reeve, E., "Using the Villego® Simulation to Teach The Last Planner System®," *Lean Construction Journal*, 2014, pp. 1–15) documents the arc in unusual detail. Before Round 1, teams showed "a sense of discomfort and apprehension … uncomfortable because of the uncertainty of their role," mixed with "excitement." Once the clock started, "the mood became much more intense as teams raced … tension was apparent … Both the students and the professionals became visibly frustrated when errors were made," and "when one subcontractor struggled, the others became visibly agitated." Round 1 deliberately produces "subcontractors seeking to optimize their own work at the expense of others, a high focus on cost, and a lot of blaming others when things go wrong."

The researchers named a specific obstacle — **"pull-planning paralysis"** — in which Last Planners trying to plan backward from the end milestone "frequently got stuck after only a few tasks … frustrated with two items; the concept of pull and deciphering the reverse order of tasks." Crucially, struggling through this discomfort *is* the point: "As the participant struggles through these uncomfortable situations, the learning deepens and solidifies within them." The overall emotional summary: participants experienced "anxiety, tension, impatience, enthusiasm, and excitement" — framed as Pekrun's (2006) activity-related "achievement emotions" that drive learning, "for better or for worse."

A vivid Round-1 moment captures the experiential learning of quality-at-source: a student who had built over another's covered-up mistake admitted, "I saw [the mistake] but didn't say anything," provoking teammates to shout "you should have spoken up!"

#### How the game makes people SEE waste without labeling it
Waste is made physically visible: bricks pile up in inventory buffers (overproduction, excess inventory); workers walk back and forth fetching parts (transportation, motion); some stand idle waiting for a signal (waiting); houses are built in colors the customer didn't order (overproduction + defects); rejected builds get stuck or reworked (defects). The facilitator deliberately "looks disappointed when a house is not delivered" to make the customer's dissatisfaction felt in the room. Only in the debrief are these named as the 7/8 wastes — by which point the participants have already *seen* them.

#### The learning theory that makes it work
- **Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle (1984)** is the macro structure: round → debrief (reflective observation) → concept (abstract conceptualization) → next round (active experimentation), run 3–6 times.
- **Situated cognition / constructivism.** The Villego authors write that "learning occurs as incoming information is built upon or added to one's prior knowledge (Bransford, Brown, & Cocking, 2000; Cobb, 1994; Vygotsky, 1978)" and describe the simulation as "a form of situated cognition allowing the learner to 'construct meaning from their experiences through doing' (Brown, 2001)."
- **Achievement-emotion theory (Pekrun, 2006).** The emotional intensity is not noise; it is the carrier of the learning.
- **Learning as change (Alexander, Schallert & Reynolds, 2009).** The explicit aim is to "reconstruct management's paradigms … with the intent to change behavior."

A sobering caveat the same research surfaces: skepticism can block learning. One older superintendent objected during the LPS briefing and, by Round 2, "had mentally 'checked out' … His tightly held paradigms were too strong to break."

#### What participants should take away to their real work
Reduce batch sizes; move toward one-piece/continuous flow; pull from customer demand rather than push to forecast; limit WIP; balance the line to takt time; build quality in at the source; make work visible (kanban); collaborate across handoffs with reliable promises; and treat improvement as continuous (kaizen). The deepest takeaway is the distinction between *applying practices and understanding principles* — kanban alone won't fix a fundamentally broken system.

### ASPECT 2 — THE GAMEPLAY: How it actually works

#### Variant A — The classic Lego Lean Game (Sato & Trindade; build LEGO "houses")
The most widely shared free version was created by **Danilo Sato and Francisco Trindade (then at ThoughtWorks)**, first run at Agiles 2008 (Buenos Aires) and published as Sato, D. & Trindade, F. (2009), "The Lego Lean Game," in *Agile Processes in Software Engineering and Extreme Programming* (XP 2009), Springer LNBIP vol. 31. It is now distributed CC-licensed via iSixSigma.
- **Setup:** 8–24 participants in four teams; two facilitators. Per their scoring slides, "1 Piece = $1.00 / 1 House = $25.00" (16 bricks per house → a notional $9 profit). Five inventory stations labeled A–E with defined starting contents (A = bucket of bricks; B = pieces sorted by color; C = pieces sorted by color and size; D = a complete house set; E = finished/market). Materials: bricks in 5 sizes and 4 colors, color cards to represent market demand, index cards, a flip chart, and a countdown alarm.
- **Roles:** Team 1 sorts bricks by color (A→B); Team 2 sorts by color and size (B→C); Team 3 picks the set for one house (C→D); Team 4 builds the house and launches it to market (D→E). A facilitator plays the disappointed customer.
- **Round structure and what changes between rounds:**
  - **Iteration 1 — Push:** four rounds of 30–40 seconds. Teams work as fast as possible; the demand color card is drawn at the *end* of each round. Expected outcome: rushing, chaos, piles of WIP in inventories, few houses delivered, wrong colors built.
  - **Iteration 2 — Pull/Kanban:** each inventory gets a minimum buffer; the demand card is drawn at the *start* and shown only to Team 4; empty space on a downstream table is the kanban signal to produce. Expected outcome: calmer, less rush, some teams idle, roughly one house per round delivered.
  - **Iteration 3 — Work cell / one-piece flow:** inventories are removed; each person builds a whole house from a pile and raises a hand when done. Teaches multiskilling vs. specialization; the first house is usually done within about a minute.
  - **Iteration 4 — Kaizen (long version only):** teams design their own process, set their own buffers, run one team at a time, then reflect and improve.
- **Metrics tracked:** inventory size per station over rounds; houses built vs. houses actually bought by the market; defects; profit/loss (houses sold × $25 − bricks bought × $1); time-to-first-house.
- **Customer's role:** draws demand color cards; buys only matching houses; visibly disappointed at non-delivery — embodying "value is defined by the customer."
- **Timer:** a countdown alarm ends each round; work stops and inventory is counted.
- **Debrief structure:** a retrospective after each iteration. Debrief 1 = the 7 wastes, push vs. pull, kanban/WIP. Debrief 2 = unlevelled process, systems thinking, work cells, one-piece flow. Debrief 3 = kaizen and continuous improvement. Conclusion = profit math across rounds, "practices vs. principles," and a manufacturing-vs-software-development comparison (waterfall = push, agile = pull; a story wall = a kanban WIP-limiting device).
- **Agendas:** short version 1.5 hours; long version 3 hours.

#### Variant A-adjacent — Velaction, FlowCar, the 3L game, MTa Stockless Challenge
- **Velaction "Vel-ActionKart console":** batches reduced from 6 → 3 → 1, then two kaizen rounds (first move stations into a cell, then free improvement); roles include workstation operators, a material handler who transports batches, and "waste watchers"/observers. Needs ≥7 people; ~1.5–2 hours; kit builds ~90 models.
- **FlowCar (leanshopping.com):** build 20 LEGO fire trucks; Round 1 is a traditional functional layout with batch production producing bottlenecks, stress, high WIP, poor quality, and dissatisfied customers; later rounds introduce the 7 wastes, 5S, cell layout/single-piece flow, kanban, line balance, and standardized work. 6–9 participants; tracks duration, goods-at-work (WIP), quality, and profit via an Excel results file; the kit deliberately includes 30 "poor quality" bricks and 2 customer-change bricks.
- **3L "Learning Lean with LEGO" (academic, Brazil):** levels progressively add 5S, TPM, kanban/pull, and leveling; uses an 8-second takt time, simulates a 20-second machine breakdown, and lets teams make different kanban-placement decisions so they "feel the impact of their own decisions." Validated with statistically significant learning gains (Wilcoxon signed-rank p < 0.05) and >85% motivation scores.
- **MTa "Stockless Challenge":** build a "Small Trolley" with 6 color variants and 4 sub-assemblies; contrasts traditional manufacturing vs. one-piece flow using kanbans; teams of 3–5; structured phase-review questions; teams discover their own takt time.

#### Variant B — Villego (now Plan2Flow): the Last Planner pull-planning simulation
- **Origin/purpose:** Developed by En& (Systems thinking at work), BOB bv, and The Change Business Ltd in December 2009 to teach the Last Planner System (LPS) as a comprehensive Plan-Do-Check-Act simulation; described as "a three-hour simulation that uses Legos" focused on LPS (Hadavi, Northwestern Engineering, 2023). Now rebranded **Plan2Flow**. Last Planner® and Last Planner System® are trademarks of the Lean Construction Institute.
- **Setup:** teams of 7–14 (one kit serves 7–14); build a complex LEGO building. Eight specified roles (playable by fewer people via doubling-up): four foremen (floor, level 1, level 2, roof) plus general contractor, client/change representatives, quality controller, and data collectors. ~4–6 hours total (often spread over two days; can be split into three 90–100-minute sessions). Recommended facilitation: one facilitator + one assistant per team; facilitators should observe the game run twice before facilitating.
- **Round structure:**
  - **Round 1 — Traditional/push:** the team plans with a Gantt schedule/critical path and drawings; the facilitator gives no guidance; work is pushed; cooperation and information-sharing are low; cost is the focus; behavior is "reactive and adversarial."
  - **Between rounds:** introduce the LPS; LCI's Parade of Trades is often run here.
  - **Round 2 — LPS/pull:** collaborative pull planning *from the end milestone backward* using colored Post-it notes on a weekly calendar board; each subcontractor (color/trade) negotiates handoffs with its predecessor ("reliable promising"); after each "construction minute" the facilitator helps the team reflect and replan, writing Percent Plan Complete (PPC) on arrow-shaped sticky notes.
- **Metrics tracked:** construction time, productivity (elements/minute), number of subcontractors on site, delivery penalties, safety penalties, damaged-material-reuse penalties, waste penalties (excess LEGO studs), profit/loss, and PPC per minute. As the authors put it, "Note the differences between rounds in construction time, productivity, and profit (or loss)."
- **Representative results (Warcup & Reeve, 2014):**
  - UVU Construction Management students (one team of 10): Round 1 = 11:31 and a **loss of $117,922** → Round 2 = 5:33 and a **profit of $84,023**; productivity 8.86 → 18.38 elements/min; subcontractors on site 96 → 50; delivery penalties 8 → 0.
  - Professionals, Team 1: 12:27 / −$78,286 → 4:37 / +$119,587; productivity 8.19 → 22.09.
  - Professionals, Team 2: 12:05 / −$93,721 → 4:55 / +$13,034; productivity 8.44 → 20.75.
  - Across a separate sample of nine teams (Tsao, Draper & Howell, IGLC-22, 2014), the average Round 1 result was a **€380,000 loss** vs. a **€40,000 profit** in Round 2, with build time dropping from 21:45 to 6:22.
  - Survey: pre-simulation, only ~48% of 31 participants mentioned collaboration/planning as important; post-simulation, 76% named communication/collaboration/teamwork/commitment as key takeaways and 71% said they would implement LPS immediately.
- **Customer's role:** client/change representatives introduce design changes and customization mid-build (simulating real change orders); the GC and owner inspect for quality and individually reject flaws for rework.
- **Debrief structure:** compare Round 1 vs. Round 2 data; discuss reliable promising and collaboration; surface that "plans are models of the real world and consequently can never capture all of its detail," so teams must replan as new information emerges (the PDCA learning loop).

#### Variant C — LEAPCON (Technion Lean Apartment Construction)
- **Origin/purpose:** Developed by Rafael Sacks and Maxim Goldin at the Technion (Sacks et al., 2005, 2007), and explicitly inspired by the Airplane Game (per Rybkowski et al., 2012), to test a Lean management model for high-rise apartment finishing. Used at 71 universities and 41 companies in 37 countries; free for academic use.
- **Setup:** simulates an 8-story building, 4 apartments per floor (32 apartments), each apartment a small LEGO model; four successive trades shown as colors (flooring = orange, partitions = blue, HVAC = yellow, plaster ceiling = brown). ~10 players: project manager, client change manager, quality controller, tower-crane operator, and four specialty subcontractors. Eight apartment designs (standard A plus variants B–H). ~1h45m minimum.
- **Round structure:** Round 1 = push, batch of a full floor (4 apartments) before the next trade starts, a single subcontractor per floor, build bottom-up, whole-floor inspection with individual rework of flaws. Round 2 = pull with single-apartment flow, multiskilled crews flexibly assigned, re-sequencing allowed. After ~1 minute of play, client reps draw random apartment/design pairs from a hat to model customization arriving unpredictably.
- **The three interventions tested:** (1) reduce batch size to single apartments, (2) multiskilled teams instead of specialists, (3) pull scheduling instead of push. Across 11 live runs the Lean model increased throughput, improved cash flow, and reduced apartment delivery cycle time; Sacks (2020) notes Lean principles can roughly double throughput.
- **Metrics:** cycle time, cash flow, WIP, throughput — often visualized as Line-of-Balance charts.

#### Variant D — Parade of Trades (the companion concept game; dice, not LEGO)
- **Origin/purpose:** Choo, H.J. & Tommelein, I.D. (1999), "Parade of Trades: A Computer Game for Understanding Variability and Dependence," Technical Report 99-1, UC Berkeley (with Greg Howell and David Riley; IGLC-6, 1998). The term was "coined by Gus Sestrup, a superintendent with Turner Construction," and the game was first hosted by Greg Howell at the 6th Annual Conference of the International Group for Lean Construction (IGLC-6), Guarujá, Brazil. It is LCI's foundational simulation for the impact of **variability and dependence** on throughput in a serial, hand-off-based process.
- **Setup:** teams of seven trades process 35 chips (e.g., hospital rooms) passed down a line; dice with an average roll of 3.5 determine each trade's weekly capacity; dice and chips move in opposite directions. In the computer version the program "automatically perform[s] the rolling and passing process until all 100 units are passed through the parade of trades." Higher-variability dice (e.g., faces of 1 and 9) versus consistent dice (all 5s) produce dramatically different completion times.
- **Learning objectives:** reducing workflow variability improves total system performance, makes outcomes predictable, and simplifies coordination; "point speed and productivity do not matter — throughput does"; when flow is unpredictable, specialists self-protect by adding contingency and holding back labor to maintain utilization.
- **Debrief prompts (deliberately visceral):** "Who is the bottleneck holding everybody up? How does it feel to be the bottleneck?" and, when a trade rolls high but has no chips to process: "You brought out enough workers to complete 6 rooms … Did you lose money that week? Do you experience this on your projects?"
- **Companion games in the LCI/IGLC family:** the DPR Pull Planning Game (build a block tower planned backward with "I Get / I Give" sticky notes; each person is both customer and supplier); LEBSCO (a LEGO Last-Planner game, González et al., University of Auckland); Silent Squares (collaboration / "I've got mine" → "we've got ours"); and the Airplane Game (Visionary Products Inc., 2007 — LEGO airplanes, three rounds of six minutes, push → pull/one-piece flow).

#### Variant E — Scotland & Freudenberg's LEGO Flow Game (Agile/Kanban)
- **Setup:** teams of ≥5 build LEGO advent-calendar items; roles = Analyst (finds the next numbered door, writes an index card), Supplier (staples the matching bag of bricks to the card), Builder (assembles per the picture), Acceptor (quality-checks against the picture), Manager (times and counts WIP every 30 seconds), and optional Market reps (independent quality check). 24 items per kit.
- **Three rounds, three policies:**
  - **Batch & stage-driven (waterfall-like):** one 6-minute round; a whole batch (e.g., 5) must clear each stage before passing on; specialists can't help each other; rejected work is stuck where it is.
  - **Time-boxed (Scrum-like):** three 2-minute timeboxes; estimate/plan, then review each box; items pass on individually; collaboration and rework are allowed.
  - **Flow-based (Kanban-like):** one 6-minute round; a WIP limit of 1 per stage creates a pull system — a stage may pull a new item only when it is empty. (The WIP limit of 1 is intentionally extreme to provoke reflection; the WIP limit is per stage, not per person, so teammates can swarm to help on the in-progress item.)
- **Metrics/debrief:** the Manager counts items per stage every 30 seconds, fed into a spreadsheet that produces **Cumulative Flow Diagrams** comparing the three rounds; doors are reshuffled between rounds so the Analyst's job doesn't get easier. The goal is to reflect on the pros/cons of each approach, not to crown a winner. Common adaptations: drop the Supplier role, add a self-improvement round, introduce mid-round requirement changes, or use blind-bag minifigures or the Ubongo puzzle game instead of advent calendars.

## Recommendations

**Stage 1 — Choose the variant to fit your audience and concept (do this first).**
- For general Lean/operations or office teams learning **flow vs. batch, push vs. pull, WIP, kanban**: run the **Sato/Trindade Lego Lean Game** (free, CC-licensed) or a turnkey kit (Velaction, FlowCar). Budget 1.5–3 hours.
- For **Agile/software/knowledge-work** teams: run **Scotland's LEGO Flow Game** (waterfall vs. Scrum vs. Kanban; produces Cumulative Flow Diagrams). 60–90 minutes.
- For **construction/AEC** teams learning **pull planning and the Last Planner System**: run **Villego/Plan2Flow** (~3–6 hours) or **LEAPCON** (~2 hours), and precede it with the **Parade of Trades** to establish *why* variability matters.

**Stage 2 — Design the rounds as a deliberate Kolb loop.** Always run a "bad" Round 1 (batch + push) and a "good" later round (flow + pull), with a structured debrief between each. Never skip the debrief — undebriefed rounds are entertainment, not learning. Run the loop 2–4 times; more than that fatigues participants (the construction-simulation literature warns that energy and focus fade after about three hours).

**Stage 3 — Capture hard numbers and show the gap.** Track lead time / time-to-first-unit, throughput, WIP (count the piles), defects/penalties, customer satisfaction (delivered vs. demanded), and profit/loss. The before/after contrast (loss → profit, time halved) is the single most persuasive artifact. Pre-empt the "we just practiced" objection with the Airplane-Game finding that ~70% of gains come from Lean principles and <30% from the learning curve/Hawthorne effect.

**Stage 4 — Facilitate for discovery, not lecture.** Let participants name the wastes themselves; ask experiential questions ("How did it feel to be the bottleneck?"). Engineer the emotional arc deliberately (play the disappointed customer; let the push round get chaotic). Expect and *welcome* friction such as "pull-planning paralysis" — it is precisely where the learning solidifies.

**Stage 5 — Bridge to real work.** End every session by mapping each discovery to participants' actual processes ("Where do you batch today? Where does WIP pile up? Who is your bottleneck?") and to one or two concrete experiments. Emphasize principles over practices.

**Benchmarks that should change your approach:**
- If participants leave able to *recite* the wastes but cannot *point to them in their own process*, your debrief is too abstract — strengthen the bridge-to-work step.
- If a senior skeptic "checks out" (as documented in the Villego study), the simulation alone won't convert them; pair it with data from their own operation.
- Facilitator ratios matter: ~1 facilitator per 1–3 teams for manufacturing flow games; 1 facilitator (plus an assistant) per team for Villego.

## Caveats
- **One-piece flow is not universally optimal.** Practitioners note that for very short processes, or where transport/changeover between steps is expensive, smaller *batches* (not literal one-piece flow) are the right answer. The game's WIP limit of 1 is intentionally extreme to provoke reflection, not a prescription — good debriefs ask "What is a *sensible* batch size / WIP limit here?"
- **Game results are single-instance and can be noisy.** The Villego scorecards are not statistically tested; one team's waste penalties actually *rose* between rounds (16 → 172 studs) and one printed time figure is ambiguous ("4.37" vs. 4:37). Warcup & Reeve is a qualitative case study with only two groups (one 10-student team; 21 professionals in two teams), and the authors state results "may not be precisely generalized." Treat the numbers as illustrative of direction and rough magnitude, not as guaranteed outcomes.
- **Outcomes depend on attitude and facilitation.** The same research shows learning is mediated by each participant's emotions and attitude; a deeply skeptical participant may learn little. Facilitation quality (ratio, debrief skill, having observed the game run beforehand) materially affects results.
- **Commercial vs. free.** The Sato/Trindade game, Scotland's LEGO Flow Game, LEAPCON (academic use), and the Parade of Trades are free or low-cost; Villego/Plan2Flow, Velaction, FlowCar, and ELSE Inc.'s One-Piece Flow Game are commercial products. Note the rebrand: "Villego" is now "Plan2Flow," so older facilitator references use the former name.
- **Don't confuse it with LEGO® SERIOUS PLAY®.** LEGO SERIOUS PLAY is a distinct method (metaphor-building for strategy, team cohesion, and surfacing tacit knowledge) and is *not* a Lean process simulation — although both rely on the "think with the hands" principle.
